07 Trail Braking Analysis —
▼
What is trail braking? Trail braking means keeping light brake pressure past turn-in, gradually releasing it to the apex. This keeps weight on the front tyres, increasing front grip exactly when you need rotation. On the trace it looks like a gentle ramp to zero. An abrupt cliff means you dumped the brakes before the corner — losing front load and rotation at the worst moment.
08 Throttle Shape —
▼
What to look for: After the apex, throttle should build progressively — a smooth ramp from 0% to 100% as the car straightens. A snap to full throttle causes oversteer and wheelspin, losing traction at the exit. A flat/delayed pickup means you're leaving power — and speed — on the table. The ideal trace looks like a gentle S-curve.
09 Traction Circle —
▼
What is the traction circle? A tyre can only generate a fixed total amount of grip force. The circle represents that limit. Points near the edge = using maximum grip. Points in the centre = grip wasted. The ideal racing line uses combined braking-and-turning and combined throttle-and-turning — shown as smooth arcs around the edge. Two separate blobs (brake straight, then turn) means you're leaving grip unused.
10 Reference Points —
▼
Why reference points matter: Consistent lap times require the same action at the same place every lap. A brake point that moves by even 5 metres lap-to-lap will produce 0.2–0.4s variation. Telemetry shows exactly where you braked and got on throttle — expressed as lap distance %. If your brake point in T3 is at 42.1% one lap and 43.8% the next, you don't have a reference point — you have an approximation.
11 Lap-Over-Lap Learning —
▼
What deliberate practice looks like on telemetry: When you're learning a corner correctly, apex speed increases lap-over-lap as confidence builds. When you're not learning — just repeating — it stays flat. This section shows exactly which corners you improved in this session, which stayed the same, and which got worse. Use it to decide where to focus next session.
